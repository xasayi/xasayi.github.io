Designing an Optimal Optical Lens System

Creating a Laser Frequency Mode Map

Testing COVID-19 Diagnostic Strips

The samarium experiment searches for a theoretical narrow spectroscopic feature in a crystal that is lower-doped with samarium ions. However, as the desired transition includes a forbidden transition, it can only be achieved by induced magnetic state mixing. The nature of the transition results in a weak transition, thus a weak signal observed from the spontaneous emission of photons as the electrons return to their final ground state in a 3-level system. Therefore, during the summer of 2020 while I was working in AMO physics at Vuthalabs, I performed a number of simulations in Zemax Optical Studio to determine an optimal lens configuration to increase the number of photons that can hit the vacuum chamber window after shining a laser on the samarium crystal. Analysis was performed on how lens addition and combinations can increase the photomultiplier cube collector efficiency compared to the original setup. CADing was also done to make key components of the collection lens system to fit it on the vacuum cryostat. At the end, a theoretical efficiency of 8x greater than the original efficiency is achieved. The images on the left show the lens system with the vacuum cryostat and the Zemax simulations of light rays.

Oftentime, it is hard to maintain the stability of a laser lasing at a specific frequency during an experiment when the laser temperature and current changes. To find a stable region where the laser frequency will not fluctuate and suddenly experience mode hops from the desired frequency, I created a laser mode geography map during the 2020-2021 school year while working at Vuthalabs. This would allow a relatively time efficiency way of scanning over a range of temperature and current to map out the changes in laser frequency. Furthermore, an automated program is created with respondents to the map to automatically tell one the regions of interest. For example, the image on the right shows regions where the frequency is within 5Hz of the desired frequency.

The current COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the urgent need for rapid high-sensitivity diagnostic testing for large-scale population screening. For summer 2021, I will be working with Prof. Liu’s microfluidics and BioMEMS lab at the University of Toronto to manufacture and test such lateral flow test strips. I will be developing the manufacturing facility for lab fabrication of flow test strips and manufacturing as well as calibrating the lateral flow test strips for detection of N proteins in SARS-CoV-2 in spiked saliva samples.
